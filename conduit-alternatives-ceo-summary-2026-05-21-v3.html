§01 The Call

Request quotes from Duve and Akia. Decide on data, not reputation.

Recommendation
The Guesty → Mews migration removes our unified inbox. We need to replace it across all 137 listings — every hotel room and every vacation rental. Two vendors clear the bar on functionality: Duve and Akia. Both require a sales conversation to price properly against our footprint. Request quotes from both this week, assess on cost + Mews-integration depth + AI quality, then pilot the winner on Edificio Poem at its cutover.

Guesty's unified inbox — the one place our team handles guest messages from Airbnb, Booking.com, Expedia and direct email — disappears with the Mews migration. Mews has only limited native messaging. Conduit was the obvious replacement on paper, but the quote came in too high, so we walked. The market has two viable alternatives with a working three-OTA inbox today (Airbnb's 2023 API change ruled out most others). The right move is a real RFQ to both, then a four-week pilot of whichever quotes better.

§02 Do Boutique Hotels Use Airbnb?

Yes — and the trend points sharply upward.

Booking.com is non-negotiable for any European hotel — that's the easy half. The harder question is whether Airbnb is worth wiring up for our four hotels. The 2025 industry data says yes, especially in our market.

"Airbnb is going significantly more aggressively into hotels," targeting bed-and-breakfasts, inns, and boutique hotels rather than large chains. Hotel-pilot programs launched October 2025 in New York, Los Angeles, and Madrid. — Brian Chesky, Airbnb Q4 2025 earnings call · summarised in RentalScaleUp
Channel Status for boutique hotels (2025–2026) Implication for Airnest hotels
Booking.com Near-universal for European hotels; ~25–40% of inventory revenue typical for boutique segment. Required
Expedia Mature hotel channel; strong for US/UK inbound to Mediterranean leisure markets. Required
Airbnb Hotels still single-digit % of Airbnb nights, but growing at 2× the platform rate. Operators (Sage, Arlo) report seamless tech integration and favourable commissions. Madrid is one of three global pilot cities. Strongly recommended
Airbnb · Canary Islands cohort Las Palmas listings reported 76% average occupancy June 2024 – May 2025; boutique-hotel listings already present (e.g. La Casa de La Camelia, Hotel Fonda Central) — competitor set is live on the channel. Compelling

Conclusion: none of our four hotels (Masanet, Veintiuno, Bandama, Emeté) should be off Airbnb in 2026 — and most boutique competitors in our market aren't. The vendor we pick has to handle Airbnb inbound chat for hotels just as it does for VRs.

§04 The Two Finalists

Duve and Akia — what each one is actually good at.

Both vendors integrate with Mews, both deliver the three-OTA unified inbox we need, and both have an active Airbnb API integration (which is the filter that disqualifies HelloShift, Bookboost, HiJiffy and Chatlyn post the Sept-2023 Airbnb relay shutdown). The differences are about pricing model, geography, AI depth, and where each one's product is strongest.

Vendor A · Duve
Per-room platform with deep Mews fit

Pricing model: tiered minimums ($120 Basic / $150 Pro / $200 Premium / Enterprise custom) plus per-room rate from $6/room/mo. Unified inbox lives in Pro tier and above. OTA-messaging is positioned as an add-on across all tiers, billed separately.

Strengths: ranked #2 Guest Messaging Software in the 2025 HotelTechAwards. Connects to Mews and 150+ other hospitality systems. European hospitality footprint (founded Israel, strong EU customer base). One large group cut its guest-comms team from 200 → 24 staff using Duve's automation. Implementation in ~10 days.

Weaknesses: 76% user satisfaction (good, not great). Specific reviewer complaints around subscription-management UX and support responsiveness. OTA messaging is an add-on, not bundled — quote will be the truth.

Indicative at 137 listings: €750–900/mo + add-on (RFQ)
Vendor B · Akia
AI-first messaging, bundled OTAs in entry tier

Pricing model: "Talk to Sales." Bronze → Silver → Gold → Platinum tiers without public pricing. Capterra reports a $43/mo Basic flat (likely small-property scale). Industry sources cite ~$200/property/mo for advanced plans. Implementation $500–$3,000 plus training $500–$2,500 on top.

Strengths: AI agents for early-check-in / upgrade / pricing-rule responses without staff in the loop — described as the most autonomous AI in this shortlist. Mews integration depth confirmed (15+ operations, bidirectional). OTA messaging (Airbnb + Booking + Expedia) bundled into the Basic tier per research docs. UI praised in reviews (4.3/5 customer-service rating).

Weaknesses: North-America-anchored sales motion and customer base — fewer European boutique-hotel case studies. Capterra reviews flag integration friction with some PMS systems and occasional AI auto-response errors. Pricing opacity makes apples-to-apples comparison harder.

Indicative at 137 listings: €900–1,800/mo + implementation (RFQ)

§05 Numbers

Two pricing shapes, one cost envelope.

Conduit was negotiated and rejected — we never paid for it. So this isn't a "vs Conduit" saving exercise. It's a new operating cost to backfill the inbox we lose when Guesty's PMS goes away. The cost depends heavily on which pricing model each vendor lands on, which is why the RFQ matters more than any list-price math.

Conduit anchor Flat fee at 137-listing scope (negotiated, walked away) ~€900 N/A ~€11k
Numbers are envelopes, not quotes. Duve's per-room model maps reasonably cleanly to 137 listings (treating each hotel room and VR as a "room"). Akia's per-property model is more sensitive to how they count our portfolio — 9 named properties plus 22 scattered VRs gets billed very differently depending on whether scattered VRs are bundled with the nearest property or counted as standalone. The RFQ has to spell this out or both quotes will be misleading.

The prize is operational, not financial. Either vendor will probably price within 30% of what Conduit quoted — the real difference is which one our team will actually want to use day-to-day, and which one has the AI depth to keep response latency low as we grow to 180 units in 2026. Treat the cost envelope as the floor; the quality of the integration is the ceiling.

§06 The Ask

Three decisions before Poem goes live.

  1. Approve an OTA omninbox as a 2026 ops line item.
    The Guesty → Mews migration removes our unified inbox. Without a replacement, the team handles Airbnb, Booking.com, Expedia and direct messages in four separate places — slower responses, fragmented guest history, harder Colombia-ops onboarding. The expected envelope is €750–1,800/mo across 137 listings, depending on which vendor and which pricing model wins.
  2. Authorise me to request quotes from Duve and Akia this week.
    RFQ spells out our footprint with the hotel-rooms / VR-units breakdown (~50 hotel rooms, ~87 VR units, 9 named properties + 22 scattered single VRs). Asks each vendor for: total monthly fee, OTA-add-on cost if separate, implementation + training one-time, contract length and exit terms, list of comparable European customers. Quotes back within 7–10 days. Decision matrix: 60% cost, 25% AI/integration quality, 15% reference quality. We pilot the winner.
  3. Pilot the winning vendor on Edificio Poem at its cutover.
    Poem is the next migration anyway — the pilot rides on the cutover work, no extra coordination cost. Four weeks of real-world use. Measure: OTA-message coverage across all three channels, Canarias-Spanish reply quality, staff adoption, response latency. Day 30: sign the portfolio contract if KPIs hold, or fall back to the runner-up for a second pilot if not. Roll the chosen vendor onto each subsequent property at its own Mews cutover, one per week.
Alternative — parallel pilot of both vendors: we could run Duve and Akia simultaneously on Poem for 30 days and pick on real data instead of just quote-quality. The upside is genuine: side-by-side, same property, same staff, same guests — the strongest possible decision basis, and it sharpens the pricing leverage against both vendors. The downside is real too: two vendor onboardings the same week as a property cutover, two sets of staff training, two integrations to monitor. For the Colombia ops team in the middle of the broader migration sequence, that's a meaningful load. Worth doing only if the quote-stage analysis is genuinely too close to call, or if Javier wants the maximum negotiation leverage.
